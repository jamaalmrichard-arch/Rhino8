The smarter you are, the more likely you are to make these mistakes. That sounds counterintuitive. It isn’t.

Mistake 1: Over-diversifying into mediocrity.

You have a 401(k), a Roth, some index funds, maybe an HSA, maybe some individual stocks. You’re “diversified.” But diversification before concentration is just spreading weakness thin. The wealthiest people I’ve worked with got rich by going deep in ONE asset class first. Real estate. Then they diversified. Your portfolio might look sophisticated on paper. But if everything is earning 6–8% and losing 1–2% to fees, you have a lot of accounts doing the same mediocre thing.

Mistake 2: Trusting the wrong people with large sums.

Your financial advisor works for a firm. The firm gets paid when your money stays in their funds. That’s not a conspiracy. It’s a business model. The question isn’t whether your advisor is a good person. It’s whether their incentives align with yours. If they’ve never mentioned SDIRAs, 1031 exchanges, or cost segregation studies, that tells you everything.

Mistake 3: Confusing income with wealth.

You earn well. Maybe very well. But income and wealth are not the same thing. Income is what you earn. Wealth is what you keep and deploy. I’ve met $400K/year earners with a negative net worth and $60K/year earners with $500K in rental equity. The difference is deployment, not salary.
