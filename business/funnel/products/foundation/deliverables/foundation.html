MODULE 1: YOUR FINANCIAL BLUEPRINT — BUDGET & CASH FLOW

"You can't build wealth if you don't know where your money goes."

Why a Budget Is the Foundation of Everything

Every building starts with a foundation. Not the walls. Not the roof. Not the granite countertops. The foundation.

Your budget is the concrete slab of your financial life. Every investment decision, every loan application, every opportunity that comes your way sits on top of your ability to answer one question: Where does my money go every month?

Most people can't answer that question. They know their income (roughly). They know their rent or mortgage payment. They know their car note. But the other 40-60% of their spending? It's a blur of debit card swipes, auto-pay subscriptions, and "I'll figure it out later."

Later never comes. Until now.

This module is going to teach you how to build a household Profit & Loss statement. The same document that every business in the world uses to track its financial health. Because your household IS a business. It has revenue (your income). It has expenses (everything you spend). And it has a bottom line: your cash flow, the money left over after everything is paid.

If your bottom line is negative, you're going bankrupt in slow motion. If your bottom line is positive but small, you're surviving but not building. If your bottom line is positive and growing, you're ready to deploy capital into assets that generate more income.

The goal of Module 1 is to find out which one you are. And then fix it.

Your Household P&L: Income vs. Expenses

A Profit & Loss statement has two sections:

Revenue (Income): Everything that puts money in your pocket. This includes:

  • W-2 wages (your paycheck)
  • 1099 income (freelance, contract, gig work)
  • Government benefits (VA disability, Social Security, unemployment)
  • Child support or alimony received
  • Side income (rental income, selling items, cash work)
  • Any other recurring money coming in

Use your NET income, the amount that actually hits your bank account after taxes and deductions. If you use gross income, your budget will look healthier than it actually is. That's a lie you can't afford.

Expenses: Everything that takes money out of your pocket. This is where it gets real.

The Three Types of Expenses

Every dollar you spend falls into one of three categories:

Fixed Expenses. These are the same amount every month. They don't fluctuate. Predictable and non-negotiable (mostly).

  • Rent or mortgage payment
  • Car payment
  • Car insurance
  • Phone bill (if on a flat-rate plan)
  • Streaming subscriptions
  • Gym membership
  • Loan payments (student loans, personal loans)
  • Child care (flat monthly rate)

Variable Expenses. These change every month. Hardest to predict, easiest to overspend on.

  • Groceries
  • Gas
  • Dining out
  • Entertainment
  • Clothing
  • Personal care (haircuts, beauty products)
  • Gifts
  • Cash withdrawals (ATM)

Hybrid Expenses. These occur every month but the amount fluctuates. They behave like fixed expenses with variable amounts.

  • Electric bill
  • Water bill
  • Gas/heating bill
  • Internet bill (if usage-based)
  • Medical co-pays

Pro tip for hybrid expenses: Pull your last 12 months of utility bills. Find the highest month. Budget that amount every month. When the bill comes in lower, the difference stays in your account as a buffer. When the bill spikes, you're already prepared.

The 90-Day Bank Statement Exercise

Here is where the real work begins.

Pull your last 90 days of bank statements. Every account. Checking, savings, credit cards, PayPal, Venmo, Cash App. Every place money moves through.

Go through every single transaction and categorize it:

  • Fixed, Variable, or Hybrid
  • Necessary or Discretionary

"Necessary" means your life or livelihood depends on it: rent, food, transportation to work, utilities. "Discretionary" means it's a choice: dining out, entertainment, subscriptions, shopping.

This exercise will take you 2-3 hours. It will be tedious. It will also be the most financially productive 3 hours of your year.

What you're looking for:

  1. Subscriptions you forgot about. The $14.99 streaming service you signed up for six months ago and watched once. The $9.99 app subscription running in the background. The $49 monthly box subscription you keep meaning to cancel.

  2. Lifestyle creep. Compare your spending 90 days ago to your spending last month. Are your restaurant bills increasing? Are your Amazon orders increasing? When your income goes up, your spending tends to follow. Silently. Without your permission.

  3. Phantom expenses. Fees, penalties, overdraft charges, interest payments on credit cards you meant to pay off. These are dollars that produce zero value in your life and exist only because of disorganization.

Getting Lender-Ready: Your Document Checklist

Before you ever look at a property, your financial documents need to be organized and ready. Good deals move fast. You can't afford to spend 3 weeks hunting down paperwork. The person with their documents ready gets the property. The person scrambling to find last year's tax return watches the deal go to someone else.

Here is what every lender will ask for. Gather these now, not later.

CHECKLIST: Lender-Ready Document File

  • Two most recent pay stubs. Current employer, showing year-to-date earnings.
  • Last 2 years of federal tax returns. Complete returns, all pages, all schedules. If you're self-employed, include Schedule C, Schedule E, and any K-1 forms.
  • IRS Tax Transcripts. Request from irs.gov (Form 4506-T). Lenders verify your tax returns against IRS records. If they don't match, your loan is dead.
  • W-2s from the last 2 years. From every employer.
  • Last 2 months of bank statements. Every account (checking, savings, money market). All pages, even the blank ones. Lenders look for large deposits that aren't from your regular income. Those require written explanation.
  • Credit reports from all three bureaus. Experian, Equifax, TransUnion. Pull from annualcreditreport.com (free, once per year). Review for errors, collections, and balances.
  • Photo ID. Government-issued, non-expired.
  • Social Security card or documentation
  • Letters of explanation (if applicable). Gaps in employment, large deposits, derogatory credit items. Write these now. Each letter should explain: what happened, when it happened, and why it won't happen again. One paragraph. Direct.
  • Proof of any additional income. Rental income (existing leases), child support court orders, VA disability award letters, Social Security award letters, pension statements.

Store everything in one folder, digital (Google Drive, Dropbox) and physical (a folder you can hand to a loan officer). Label it "Lender File." Update it every 90 days.

In the audio for this module, I walk you through filling out the Budget P&L with a real example. Every line item, every category. I also share the conversation I have with every new client about why this document is the single most important piece of paper in their financial life.

MODULE 2: RISK MANAGEMENT — PROTECTING WHAT YOU BUILD

"You don't build a house and then hope a storm never comes. You build the house to survive the storm."

The Pyramid of Needs

Before you invest a single dollar, you need to understand a framework that most financial advisors will never teach you. Why? Because it doesn't make them any money.

It's called the Pyramid of Needs, and it works like this:

         /\
        /  \
       / WP \       LEVEL 3: Wealth Preservation & Distribution
      /------\      (Estate planning, trusts, tax optimization)
     /   WA   \     LEVEL 2: Wealth Accumulation
    /----------\    (Investing, real estate, business building)
   /    RM      \   LEVEL 1: Risk Management
  /--------------\  (Insurance, emergency fund, asset protection)

Level 1: Risk Management. The foundation. Insurance coverage, emergency reserves, liability protection. This is the base that everything else sits on.

Level 2: Wealth Accumulation. Investing in assets that generate income and grow in value. Real estate, businesses, retirement accounts.

Level 3: Wealth Preservation & Distribution. Estate planning, trusts, tax strategy, generational wealth transfer. This is where you protect and pass on what you built.

Here is the mistake 90% of people make: they skip Level 1 and go straight to Level 2.

They start investing before they have adequate insurance. They buy rental properties before they have an LLC. They put money into the stock market while carrying zero disability coverage. Then ONE event wipes out everything they built. A car accident. A lawsuit. A disability. A death in the family.

The Pyramid of Needs says: secure the base before you build the tower. Risk management comes FIRST. Not because it's exciting. Not because it makes you money. Because without it, everything else is a house of cards.

This module walks you through every layer of risk management you need before you invest your first dollar. This is the rebar inside the concrete. You'll never see it, but it's the reason the building stands.

Life Insurance: The Conversation Nobody Wants to Have

Let me ask you a question most people avoid: if you died tomorrow, what happens to the people who depend on you?

Can your spouse pay the mortgage? Can your kids stay in their school? Can your parents cover their bills without the help you provide?

And the flip side: if someone YOU depend on died tomorrow, a spouse, a parent, a business partner, a co-signer, what happens to your financial stability?

Life insurance is not about you. It's about the people you leave behind and the people whose absence would leave you exposed.

Group vs. Individual Policies

If you have life insurance through your employer, you probably have a group policy. Here's what most people don't know about group policies:

The portability problem. Most employer-provided group life insurance is NOT portable. That means when you leave that job, whether you quit, get fired, or get laid off, your coverage disappears. On the same day you lose your income, you also lose your life insurance. Worst possible timing.

Some group policies offer a portability rider that lets you convert your group coverage to an individual policy when you leave. But here's the catch: not all do, and even the ones that offer portability often come with higher premiums upon conversion.

The action: Call your HR department. Ask specifically:

  1. "Is my group life insurance policy portable?"
  2. "If I leave the company, can I convert to an individual policy?"
  3. "What is the conversion cost?"

If the answer to question 1 is "no," you need an individual life insurance policy that you own outside of your employer. A policy that follows YOU, not your job.

The general rule: 10-12 times your annual income. If you make $50,000 per year, you need $500,000-$600,000 in life insurance coverage.

Term life insurance (coverage for a set period, 10, 20, or 30 years) is almost always the right choice for wealth builders. It's dramatically cheaper than whole life or universal life, and it covers you during the years when your dependents need the income replacement most. A healthy 35-year-old can get $500,000 in 20-year term coverage for $25-$40 per month. That's the cost of one dinner out. To protect everything.

Short-Term Disability: The Coverage Most People Forget

Here's a statistic that should concern you: you are more likely to be disabled during your working years than you are to die. A 20-year-old worker has a 25% chance of becoming disabled before reaching age 67.

If you can't work for 3 months, 6 months, a year. Can you survive? Can you pay rent? Can you eat? Can you keep your insurance?

Short-term disability insurance replaces a portion of your income (typically 60%) when you can't work due to illness or injury. It's the bridge that keeps your financial life intact while you recover.

What You Need to Know

Elimination Period: This is the waiting period between when you become disabled and when benefits start paying. There are two types:

  • Injury: Often 0 days (coverage begins immediately after a qualifying injury)
  • Sickness: Often 7 days (coverage begins 7 days after a qualifying illness)
  • Some policies have 14/14 waiting periods. Read the fine print.

Benefit Amount: Typically 60% of your gross income. Some policies decrease the benefit amount over time, meaning you receive less per month the longer you're disabled. Read the schedule of benefits carefully.

Benefit Period: How long the policy pays. Ranges from 3 months to 2 years.

The action: If your employer offers short-term disability, review the policy details. If they don't, or if the coverage is inadequate, look into individual short-term disability policies. They're relatively affordable and the one piece of coverage you pray you never need but can't afford to be without.

THE TRUTH

You need short-term disability insurance BEFORE you start investing. The moment you put money into a rental property or an investment, that money is not liquid. It's working for you, but it can't pay your rent this month if you break your leg. Disability coverage is the safety net that lets you invest aggressively without risking your basic survival.

Health Insurance: The Math That Matters

Everyone has health insurance (or should). But almost nobody understands what their health insurance actually covers until they get a bill that stops their heart.

Let me break down the terms that matter with real math.

Deductible vs. Co-Pay: The $20,000 Broken Leg

Say you break your leg. The emergency room bill is $20,000. Here is how your insurance processes that:

Your deductible: The amount you pay before insurance covers anything. Let's say yours is $5,000.

Your co-insurance: After you hit your deductible, insurance covers a percentage, but not 100%. A common split is 80/20 (insurance pays 80%, you pay 20%).

The math:

Line Item Amount
Total bill $20,000
You pay: Deductible -$5,000
Remaining after deductible $15,000
You pay: 20% co-insurance on remaining -$3,000
Insurance pays: 80% co-insurance $12,000
Your total out-of-pocket $8,000

That $20,000 broken leg costs you $8,000. If you don't have $8,000 saved, that goes on a credit card or into medical debt. That's how medical bills become financial emergencies.

Out-of-Pocket Maximum

The one number that protects you: your annual out-of-pocket maximum. This is the most you'll pay in a calendar year, after which insurance covers 100%.

Type Typical Range (2026)
Individual $4,000 - $9,200
Family $8,000 - $18,400

Know your number. This is the worst-case scenario for any given year.

The Emergency Room Trap: In-Network vs. Out-of-Network

Here's the detail that catches people off guard, especially families.

Your insurance has a network of approved doctors, hospitals, and specialists. When you use in-network providers, you pay the negotiated (lower) rate. When you use out-of-network providers, you pay significantly more. Sometimes the full retail price.

The problem: ambulances don't check your network. They take you to the nearest hospital. If that hospital is out of network, your $20,000 bill becomes a $40,000 bill, and your insurance may cover a fraction of it.

It gets worse on vacation. Your kids fall off a bike in another state. The local urgent care isn't in your network. The nearest in-network hospital is 90 minutes away. You go to the closer one. That's a rational decision that could cost you thousands.

The action:

  1. Know your in-network hospitals within a 30-mile radius of your home.
  2. Know your out-of-network deductible and maximum (it's usually 2-3x higher than in-network).
  3. If you travel frequently, consider a PPO plan over an HMO. PPOs have broader networks.
  4. Ask your employer about supplemental accident insurance. It pays a flat benefit regardless of network, which helps cover the gap.

Property & Casualty Insurance

This category covers the physical things in your life. Your home (or apartment), your car, your belongings.

Renters Insurance

If you rent, you need renters insurance. Your landlord's insurance covers the building, not your stuff. If there's a fire, a break-in, or water damage, everything you own is unprotected unless you have renters insurance.

Cost: typically $15-$30 per month for $30,000-$50,000 in personal property coverage. That is less than a streaming subscription to protect everything you own.

Homeowners Insurance

If you own your home, your mortgage lender requires homeowners insurance. But "required" and "adequate" are two different things. Review your policy for:

  • Dwelling coverage: Does it cover the full replacement cost of your home, or only the market value? These are different numbers.
  • Personal property coverage: Is it "actual cash value" (depreciated) or "replacement cost" (what it costs to buy new)? Replacement cost is worth the extra premium.
  • Liability coverage: If someone gets hurt on your property, this protects you. Standard is $100,000, which is not enough. More on this in the landlord section.

Auto Insurance: The Deductible Strategy

Here's a counterintuitive piece of advice: raise your deductible.

Most people carry a $500 deductible on their auto insurance because it feels safe. But according to the Property Casualty Insurers Association of America, the average driver has an accident once every 18 years. You're paying a higher monthly premium every month for 18 years to avoid a one-time $500 expense.

The math:

Deductible Monthly Premium (approx.) Annual Cost
$500 $165 $1,980
$1,000 $140 $1,680
$2,500 $115 $1,380

By raising your deductible from $500 to $1,000, you save approximately $300 per year. Over 18 years (average time between accidents), you save $5,400. When you DO have an accident, you pay an extra $500 out of pocket. Net savings: $4,900.

By raising it to $2,500, you save approximately $600 per year, or $10,800 over 18 years. Even if you have two accidents in that span, you still save $5,800.

The key: Only raise your deductible if you have the deductible amount saved in your emergency fund. This is not a gamble. It's a calculated decision backed by data.

Umbrella Insurance

An umbrella policy is additional liability coverage that kicks in after your underlying policies (auto, homeowners, landlord) are exhausted. If someone sues you and wins a $500,000 judgment, and your landlord policy only has $300,000 in liability coverage, the umbrella covers the remaining $200,000.

Cost: approximately $150-$300 per year for $1 million in umbrella coverage. For real estate investors, this is non-negotiable. One slip-and-fall lawsuit on your rental property can wipe out years of cash flow without it.

Loss of Rent Coverage

If your rental property suffers damage that makes it uninhabitable (fire, storm, major plumbing failure), loss of rent coverage pays you the rental income you would have received while the property is being repaired. Without it, you're paying the mortgage on a property generating zero income.

When you buy your first rental property, tell your insurance agent: "I need a landlord policy with liability coverage of at least $300,000, loss of rent coverage, and I want to discuss an umbrella policy." That one sentence protects you from 90% of the financial risks new landlords face.

Asset Protection Basics: Why LLCs Matter for Investors

This is a brief overview, not legal advice. But you need to understand the concept before you need the structure.

When you own rental properties in your personal name, your personal assets are exposed to lawsuits related to those properties. If a tenant slips on ice at your rental and sues for $500,000, they can go after the rental property AND your personal home, your car, your savings, your other investments.

A Limited Liability Company (LLC) creates a legal barrier between your investment properties and your personal assets. The LLC owns the property. If someone sues the LLC, they can go after the LLC's assets, but not yours personally (in most cases, if the LLC is properly maintained).

When to Set Up an LLC

Not yet. If you don't own investment property, you don't need an LLC today. Setting one up too early costs money (filing fees, annual fees, registered agent fees) with zero benefit.

The right time: Before you close on your first investment property. Work with a real estate attorney to:

  1. Choose the right state for your LLC (often the state where the property is located)
  2. Draft an operating agreement
  3. Set up a separate bank account for the LLC
  4. Ensure the LLC is properly funded and maintained

The wrong time: After you already own the property in your personal name and then try to transfer it to an LLC. This can trigger a "due on sale" clause in your mortgage. Do it right from the beginning.

Your Balance Sheet: What You Own vs. What You Owe

In Module 1, you built a P&L, tracking income vs. expenses, the flow of money in and out every month. Now we build the other half of your financial picture: the balance sheet.

A balance sheet is a snapshot. It answers one question: Right now, today, what is the total value of everything you own minus everything you owe?

The answer is your net worth.

Your net worth can be positive (you own more than you owe), negative (you owe more than you own), or zero (break-even). No judgment here. The number is the number. What matters is that you know it, because you can't change a number you've never calculated.

The Four Asset Classes

Everything you own falls into one of four categories:

1. Paper Assets. Financial instruments you hold but don't physically possess.

  • Checking and savings accounts
  • Retirement accounts (401(k), IRA, Roth IRA, TSP, 403(b), pension)
  • Brokerage / investment accounts (stocks, bonds, mutual funds, ETFs)
  • HSA (Health Savings Account) balances
  • Certificates of deposit (CDs)
  • Savings bonds
  • Crypto holdings

2. Business Assets. Ownership stakes in businesses or income-producing ventures.

  • LLC ownership / business equity
  • Equipment or inventory you own for business purposes
  • Intellectual property (patents, courses, books, licensing deals)
  • Accounts receivable (money owed to you for work completed)

3. Real Estate Assets. Property you own, including your primary residence.

  • Primary home (market value minus mortgage balance = equity)
  • Investment properties
  • Land
  • Ownership interest in real estate partnerships or syndications

4. Commodity / Hard Assets. Physical items of value.

  • Gold and silver (physical or paper)
  • Vehicles (current market value, not what you paid)
  • Jewelry and watches (appraised value)
  • Collectibles (art, coins, rare items, only if appraised)
  • Cash value of life insurance policies (if applicable)

Your Liabilities: Everything You Owe

On the other side of the balance sheet:

  • Mortgage balance(s)
  • Car loan balance(s)
  • Student loan balance(s)
  • Credit card balances (every card)
  • Personal loan balances
  • Medical debt
  • Tax debt (IRS, state)
  • Money owed to family or friends
  • Any other debts or obligations

MODULE 4: YOUR RETIREMENT ACCOUNTS & THE WEALTH LEVER

"Most people have money. They have it in accounts that earn 6% while they could be earning 15-25%. They don't have an income problem. They have a deployment problem."

Your Retirement Money Is Already Working. The Question Is: For Whom?

If you've ever held a full-time job, there's a strong chance you have money sitting in a retirement account somewhere. Maybe a 401(k). Maybe an IRA. Maybe a TSP if you're military or government. Maybe a pension from a company you left 10 years ago.

That money is invested. Right now. Today. It's earning returns and paying fees. The question is whether those returns and fees are working in YOUR best interest or in the interest of the financial institutions managing the account.

Here is the typical structure:

  • Your money sits in a target-date fund or a mix of mutual funds selected from a limited menu your employer provides.
  • Those funds charge management fees (called an expense ratio) of 0.5% to 1.5% per year. On a $200,000 account, that's $1,000 to $3,000 per year in fees. Whether the fund makes money or not.
  • The average annual return for a diversified stock portfolio over the last 30 years is approximately 7-10% before fees. After fees, you're looking at 5.5-9%.
  • Those returns are subject to market volatility. In 2008, the average 401(k) lost 25-30% of its value. In 2022, the S&P 500 dropped 19%.

Meanwhile, a well-purchased rental property in a solid market can produce 15-25% cash-on-cash return, and you have direct control over the asset. You can improve it, raise rents, refinance, or sell.

I'm not telling you to drain your retirement accounts. I'm telling you to understand the math. Because the math is the lever that changes everything.

The Retirement Formula: How Many Units Do You Need?

This is the most important formula in this workbook. It turns "someday I want to retire" into a specific, concrete, actionable number.

TMI / PCF = UNR

  • TMI = Total Monthly Income needed to retire (what you need per month to cover all your expenses and live the life you want)
  • PCF = Positive Cash Flow per unit (the net monthly income from one rental property after all expenses are paid)
  • UNR = Units Needed for Retirement (how many rental properties you need to replace your income)

Example:

Variable Amount
TMI: Monthly income needed to retire comfortably $7,000
PCF: Average positive cash flow per rental unit $750
UNR = $7,000 / $750 9.33, round up to 10 units

You need 10 rental properties each producing $750/month in positive cash flow to replace a $7,000/month income.

That's not a dream. That's a math problem. And math problems have solutions.

Self-Directed IRA: Your Retirement Money, Your Rules

Here's the part that most financial advisors will never tell you. Because it takes your money out of their control.

A Self-Directed IRA (SDIRA) is a retirement account that allows you to invest in assets beyond stocks, bonds, and mutual funds. With an SDIRA, you can invest your retirement funds in:

  • Residential real estate
  • Commercial real estate
  • Private lending (be the bank)
  • Tax liens and deeds
  • Precious metals (physical gold and silver)
  • Private companies and startups
  • And more

The money grows tax-deferred (Traditional SDIRA) or tax-free (Roth SDIRA), same as any IRA. The difference is that YOU choose the investments, not a fund manager picking from a pre-approved menu.

How It Works

  1. Open an account with an SDIRA custodian (a company that specializes in self-directed retirement accounts). Major custodians include Equity Trust, Entrust Group, Advanta IRA, and uDirect IRA.
  2. Roll over funds from your existing 401(k), IRA, TSP, or other retirement account into the SDIRA. This can be done as a direct rollover (no taxes, no penalties) if handled correctly.
  3. Direct the custodian to make investments on behalf of your IRA. The IRA purchases the asset, not you personally. This is a critical distinction.
  4. All income flows back to the IRA. Rent from a property, interest from a loan, profits from a sale. It all goes back into the SDIRA, where it grows tax-deferred or tax-free.

The Math: 6% vs. 22%

DAVID'S STORY

David was a corporate project manager making $120,000 a year. He had $80,000 in a 401(k) from a previous employer, sitting in a target-date fund earning roughly 6% per year. About $4,800 annually.

He rolled that $80,000 into a Self-Directed IRA and purchased a rental property using the BRRRR strategy (Buy, Rehab, Rent, Refinance, Repeat). After the rehab and refinance, the property was producing $730 per month in net cash flow.

That's $8,760 per year on an $80,000 deployment. A 22% cash-on-cash return. All inside his SDIRA. Tax-deferred.

Compare:

  • Old 401(k) at 6%: $4,800/year (with zero control and 1.2% in fees)
  • SDIRA in real estate at 22%: $8,760/year (with full control and no management fees)

David didn't make more money. He repositioned the money he already had into a higher-performing asset. That's the wealth lever.

Important Guardrails: When NOT to Touch Retirement Money

SDIRAs are powerful. But they are not for everyone, and there are situations where you should leave your retirement money alone:

  1. If your current employer offers a 401(k) match and you're not maxing it out. An employer match is free money, a guaranteed 50-100% return on your contribution. Max the match first.

  2. If you don't have an emergency fund. Moving retirement money into an illiquid investment (like real estate) while having no emergency reserves is reckless. Build your emergency fund first (3-6 months of expenses).

  3. If you don't understand real estate investing yet. The SDIRA is a vehicle. If you don't know how to drive, the vehicle doesn't matter. Complete this workbook first. Build your team (Module 5). Learn how to analyze deals (Module 6). Then consider the SDIRA.

  4. If the amount is too small to purchase property. With less than $30,000-$50,000 in retirement savings, the SDIRA may not give you enough capital to acquire and rehab a property. In that case, keep contributing, let it grow, and revisit when the balance is sufficient.

  5. If you're close to retirement age and need the stability. If you're 55+ and planning to retire within 5-7 years, shifting retirement funds into real estate (which is illiquid and requires a longer time horizon) may not be appropriate for your situation.

The SDIRA is a lever. But you need a solid base to pull it from. That base is everything you've built in Modules 1-3.

MODULE 5: BUILDING YOUR INVESTMENT TEAM

"You are the average of the 5 people you spend the most time with. In real estate, you are the average of the 5 professionals on your team. Choose wisely."

Why Your Team Matters More Than Your Deal

I've seen people find the perfect deal and lose it because their lender was slow. I've seen people buy a good property and destroy the profit because their contractor was dishonest. I've seen people get sued because their attorney missed something in the contract.

Your team is the difference between a good deal and a disaster. You don't need dozens of people. You need the right eight.

The 8 People You Need on Your Team

1. Real Estate Agent (Investor-Friendly)

This is NOT the same person who helps your cousin buy their first home. You need an agent who understands investment properties, can run comps for rental income (not retail resale), knows distressed properties, and is comfortable submitting offers well below asking price.

How to find one:

  • Attend your local Real Estate Investors Association (REIA) meeting. Ask other investors who they use.
  • Search BiggerPockets forums for agent recommendations in your market.
  • Call a property management company and ask which agents send them the most investor clients.

3 questions before you hire one:

  1. "How many investment property transactions did you close in the last 12 months?" (If the answer is fewer than 10, keep looking.)
  2. "What is the average cash flow on the rental properties you've helped investors buy?" (If they don't know what cash flow means, keep looking.)
  3. "Are you comfortable submitting offers significantly below asking price?" (If they hesitate, keep looking.)

Red flags:

  • They primarily work with retail home buyers, not investors
  • They discourage you from offering below asking
  • They push you toward newer, turnkey properties instead of value-add opportunities
  • They can't explain cap rate, cash-on-cash return, or ARV

2. Mortgage Broker / Lender

A good mortgage broker has access to multiple lenders and can shop your loan across different programs to find the best terms. This is different from a bank loan officer, who can only offer products from their specific bank.

How to find one:

  • Ask your investor-friendly agent for referrals.
  • Ask at REIA meetings.
  • Look for brokers who advertise investor loans (DSCR loans, portfolio loans, hard money).

Ask these 3 questions:

  1. "What types of investment property loans do you offer?" (You want to hear: conventional, FHA, DSCR, portfolio, hard money. Options matter.)
  2. "What is your average close time for investment property loans?" (Under 30 days is good. Over 45 is a problem.)
  3. "Can you pre-approve me today so I'm ready when I find a deal?" (If they cannot move quickly, your deal will go to someone else.)

Red flags:

  • They only offer conventional loans and are unfamiliar with investor-specific products
  • They take 45+ days to close
  • They can't explain the difference between a primary residence loan and an investment property loan

3. General Contractor / Handyman

You need someone who can scope repairs, provide accurate bids, complete work on time, and not disappear mid-project. This is the hardest team member to find and the most important to vet.

How to find one:

  • Drive through neighborhoods where renovations are happening. Stop and ask the crew who they work for.
  • Ask other investors at REIA meetings.
  • Check local contractor licensing boards for verified, insured contractors.
  • Do NOT rely on Angi (formerly Angie's List) or HomeAdvisor. Those leads are sold to multiple contractors, and the quality control is weak.

Vet them with these 3 questions:

  1. "Can you provide references from 3 investment property renovations you've completed in the last 6 months?" (Not home remodels for homeowners. Investment properties. Different scope, different budget discipline.)
  2. "Are you licensed and insured? Can I see your certificate of insurance?" (If they hesitate, walk away.)
  3. "How do you handle change orders and cost overruns?" (The best contractors communicate overruns immediately, not after the money is spent.)

Red flags:

  • They want a large deposit upfront (more than 10-15% of total job cost)
  • They can't provide a detailed, line-item scope of work
  • They don't have insurance
  • They have no references from investment property work
  • They push back when you ask for a fixed bid instead of time-and-materials

4. Property Manager

If you plan to manage your own properties, you still need a property manager in your contacts. Life changes. Deals in other markets come up. You get to 5+ units and self-management becomes a full-time job. Have the relationship ready before you need it.

How to find one:

  • Search for property management companies in your target market that specialize in single-family rental homes or small multifamily.
  • Ask other investors who they use and what their experience has been.

3 questions to ask:

  1. "What is your management fee and what does it include?" (Typical: 8-12% of collected rent. Clarify: does this include tenant placement, or is that a separate fee?)
  2. "What is your average vacancy rate across your managed properties?" (Under 5% is excellent. Over 10% is a concern.)
  3. "How do you handle maintenance requests, and what is your threshold for spending without owner approval?" (You want clear communication and a defined spending cap, typically $200-$500 per incident before calling you.)

Red flags:

  • Fees are unclear or have hidden charges (setup fees, lease renewal fees, inspection fees)
  • They can't tell you their vacancy rate
  • They manage too many units per employee (over 150:1 is a warning sign)
  • They don't have a tenant screening process with defined criteria

MODULE 6: YOUR BUY BOX & FIRST PROPERTY CRITERIA

"A buy box is your set of non-negotiable rules for what you will and will not buy. Without one, you chase every deal. With one, deals chase you."

What Is a Buy Box and Why Does It Matter?

A buy box is a set of criteria that defines exactly what type of property you're looking for, and more importantly, what you're NOT looking for. It's a filter. It eliminates 90% of properties from your consideration so you can focus your time, energy, and money on the 10% that match your strategy.

Without a buy box, you waste months looking at properties that don't fit your goals. You get emotionally attached to a "good deal" that's actually terrible for your strategy. You submit offers on everything and close on nothing.

With a buy box, every property that comes across your desk gets a simple yes or no. Does it match the criteria? Analyze it. Doesn't match? Next.

Here's the difference between this workbook and what most people teach: I'm not going to give you MY buy box and tell you to use it. My buy box is built for my markets, my capital, my experience level, and my strategy. Your buy box will be different. And it SHOULD be different.

I'm going to teach you how to BUILD your own.

How to Build Your Buy Box: The Framework

Step 1: Choose Your Market

Your market dictates everything. Price ranges, rent levels, appreciation potential, tenant demand, competition. Here's what to evaluate:

Population growth: Is the area gaining or losing people? Growth means demand for housing. Decline means vacancies.

Job market: Are major employers expanding or contracting? What industries drive the local economy? Diverse economies (multiple industries) are safer than single-employer towns.

Rent-to-price ratio: Take the average rent for a property type and divide by the average purchase price. A ratio of 0.8% or higher is generally favorable for cash flow. Example: $1,200 rent / $150,000 price = 0.8%.

Landlord-friendly laws: Some states and cities make eviction nearly impossible, taking 6-12 months. Others have clear, efficient processes (30-60 days). This matters enormously for your risk.

Insurance and tax costs: High property tax or high insurance states (Florida, Texas, New Jersey) eat into your cash flow. Factor these in before you compare price tags.

Step 2: Choose Your Property Type

Property Type Pros Cons Best For
Single-family home (SFR) Easiest to finance, easiest to sell, tenants treat it like their own One vacancy = 100% income loss Beginners, low capital
Duplex (2 units) House hack potential, 2 income streams More management, harder to finance as beginner Beginners with FHA loan strategy
Triplex / Fourplex 3-4 income streams, still qualifies for residential financing More management, higher price point Intermediate investors
Small multifamily (5-20 units) Economies of scale, one roof / one location Commercial financing required, higher capital Experienced investors

For most people reading this workbook, single-family homes or duplexes are the starting point. Don't overcomplicate it. Your first deal should be simple enough that you can learn from it without losing your shirt.

Step 3: Define Your Property Criteria

This is where you get specific. For each factor below, define YOUR range based on YOUR market research.

Bedrooms / Bathrooms: In most markets, 3-bed / 2-bath is the sweet spot for rental demand. Avoid 1-bed / 1-bath (limited tenant pool) and 5+ bedrooms (higher maintenance, lower demand for the cost).

Condition: How much work are you willing to take on?

  • Turnkey / Rent-ready: Little to no work needed. Higher purchase price, but immediate cash flow.
  • Light rehab: Cosmetic updates. Paint, flooring, fixtures, appliances. Lower price, fast turnaround.
  • Moderate rehab: Cosmetic + some mechanical (plumbing, electrical, HVAC). Bigger discount, more risk, more reward.
  • Heavy rehab: Gut renovation. Lowest price, highest risk, highest potential return. NOT for first-time investors.

Location factors:

  • Proximity to public transportation (expands tenant pool)
  • Proximity to major employers, hospitals, universities
  • School district quality (affects both tenant quality and resale value)
  • Crime statistics (check local police department data, not "feeling")
  • Flood zone status (check FEMA flood maps at fema.gov/flood-maps)

Step 4: Define Your Deal Killers

These are the properties you will NOT buy under any circumstances, regardless of price:

Common deal killers (and why):

  • Foundation issues: The most expensive repair. Can cost $10,000-$50,000+ and often has no permanent fix. Unless you're very experienced, walk away.
  • Flood zone properties: Flood insurance can cost $2,000-$5,000+ per year. It destroys cash flow and creates ongoing risk. Check every property against FEMA flood maps.
  • Environmental contamination: Asbestos, lead paint (pre-1978 homes, required disclosure), mold, underground storage tanks. Remediation is expensive and can create legal liability.
  • HOA restrictions on rentals: Some homeowners associations prohibit or restrict rental activity. Read the CC&Rs before you buy. If the HOA doesn't allow rentals, the deal is dead.
  • Deed restrictions or easements that limit property use.
  • Title issues that cannot be resolved before closing.

Step 5: Evaluate Repair Costs Relative to the Deal

The old approach was to set a flat cap on repairs. "$5,000 maximum." That approach eliminates 90% of the deals where real money is made. A smarter approach is to evaluate repair costs relative to the property's After Repair Value (ARV).

The 70% Rule (for wholesalers and flippers):

Maximum Offer = ARV x 70% - Repair Costs

Example: A property with an ARV of $200,000 and $40,000 in needed repairs: $200,000 x 70% = $140,000 - $40,000 = $100,000 maximum offer

This ensures you have enough margin for profit and unexpected costs.

For rental buyers (cash flow analysis):

What matters is not the absolute repair cost. It's whether the total investment (purchase + repairs) produces acceptable cash flow.
